Abstract

I build secure, privacy-preserving LLM systems at Intuitive Surgical — retrieval assistants over internal documents and code, guardrail and security analysis for LLM applications, and LLM tooling wired into CI/CD. The work sits where applied engineering meets a theorist's instinct for what a system can and cannot guarantee. [1] Secure here means concretely: keeping proprietary documents and code inside the trust boundary, and reasoning about the failure modes of a retrieval pipeline before it ships, not after.

That instinct comes from a Ph.D. in Electrical Engineering at Arizona State University (2024), advised by Prof. Gautam Dasarathy, on nonconvex optimization, graph theory, and multi-armed bandits.2 The same questions recur across both worlds: when can a learning procedure be trusted to find the right answer, how few observations does it take, and what structure in the problem makes the difference between a guarantee and a hope. [2] Earlier: research intern at MERL (2022), systems engineer at NetraDyne, and research intern at IISc. B.Tech + M.Tech, IIT Madras.

Two threads run through the publications below. The first is benign nonconvexity: many problems look hopeless — nonconvex, riddled with the threat of bad local minima — yet have a landscape on which plain gradient descent converges to the global optimum from any starting point. The second is structure as a sample-complexity multiplier: a similarity graph over the arms of a bandit, or known periodicity in a non-stationary one, lets an agent reach the right decision with far fewer observations than a structure-blind baseline.

When is a nonconvex landscape benign?

Convexity is sufficient for gradient descent to find the global minimum, but it is not necessary. A landscape can wiggle, change curvature, and still have exactly one valley — in which case descent from any initialization lands at the global optimum. That distinction is the heart of the quadratic-feasibility paper.3 The update rule is the textbook one, [3] Thaker, Dasarathy & Nedić, ISIT 2020 (arXiv:2002.01066). The result: identifiability conditions plus a benign landscape — no spurious local minima — so gradient descent converges globally.

Selected Publications

  1. Maximizing and Satisficing in Multi-armed Bandits with Graph Information (GRUB)

    P. K. Thaker, M. Malu, N. Rao, G. Dasarathy

    NeurIPS 2022 arXiv:2108.01152

    A similarity graph over the arms finds the best arm with far fewer samples than UCB — with a matching lower bound.

    arXiv →code →

    [A] The graph encodes a prior that nearby arms have similar means; sampling one arm informs its neighbors, so the algorithm spends pulls where they discriminate the most.
