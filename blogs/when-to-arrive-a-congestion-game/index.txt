14:T1b184,<p>Picture five strangers all refreshing the same flaky WiFi access point as they drive past it at 30 m/s. The hotspot blinks on and off on its own schedule; every probe drains battery; and the instant the link comes up, whoever else grabbed the same window splits the bandwidth with you. When, exactly, should you check?</p>
<p>That question — <em>when to arrive at a shared, intermittently-available resource</em> — turns out to be much harder than the textbook "when to join the queue" problem. The natural way to attack it (write down a utility, solve for its Nash equilibrium) hits a wall almost immediately. What got us through was an angle I still find a little sneaky, and it's the one move I'd want a reader to keep a week from now.</p>
<h2>What makes this harder than "when to join a queue"?</h2>
<p>The canonical ancestor is the concert-queue problem: a ticket window opens and closes at <em>fixed, known</em> times, and each customer picks a single arrival time to minimize waiting plus service. Jain et al. solved that beautifully, deriving explicit Nash-equilibrium arrival distributions. That line of work is correct and complete for what it models.</p>
<p>Three cracks open the moment you make it realistic.</p>
<p>First, the server's availability is <em>stochastic</em>. Our server is a two-state Markov chain that flips between ON and OFF, with geometric ON and OFF durations governed by two parameters: <span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math <span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>λ</mi><mi>d</mi></msub></mrow><annotation You never know when the window will open.</p>
<p>Second, the decision is <em>repeated and adaptive</em>, not single-shot. Each player <span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">ℓ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\ell</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord">ℓ</span></span></span></span> decides, every slot, whether to "sense" the server, paying a fixed cost <span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>c</mi><mi>s</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">c_s</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.5806em;vertical-align:-0.15em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal">c</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t vlist-t2"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.1514em;"><span style="top:-2.55em;margin-left:0em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mathnormal mtight">s</span></span></span></span><span class="vlist-s">​</span></span><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.15em;"><span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span> per probe. Find it OFF, you wait out the frame; find it ON, you connect.</p>
<p>Third — and this is the part the queueing literature doesn't touch — <em>many players connect at once</em>, and your payoff is inversely proportional to how many of you are connected simultaneously. Think SD versus HD video: the more of you share the ON window, the worse everyone's quality of service. Congestion shows up in the <em>payoff</em>, not by stretching the service time.</p>
<p>So each player wants three things in tension: connect quickly when the server turns ON, sense rarely (probing is expensive), and overlap with as few others as possible. We want a decentralized strategy that self-organizes to an equilibrium nobody wants to leave.</p>
<p>I'll flag what we are <em>not</em> doing: we keep congestion as payoff <span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo>∝</mo><mn>1</mn><mi mathvariant="normal">/</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mtext>connected players</mtext><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\propto 1/(\text{connected players})</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mrel">∝</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord">1/</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord text"><span class="mord">connected players</span></span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span>. The arguably more realistic processor-sharing model, where the service rate <span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>μ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mu</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.625em;vertical-align:-0.1944em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">μ</span></span></span></span> itself degrades under load, we deemed too hard and set aside. More on that at the end.</p>
<h2>Why doesn't the obvious approach work?</h2>
<p>The obvious thing is what every game theorist reaches for: write down a natural per-player utility — reward for connecting, minus sensing cost, minus a congestion penalty — and solve for its Nash equilibrium.</p>
<p>We tried. It's analytically intractable here. The repeated, stochastic, congestion-coupled structure means the "natural" utility doesn't yield a best-response map you can pin down, let alone prove a unique fixed point for. And this is genuinely <em>not</em> a congestion game — the costs aren't additive over a shared facility — so the comforting machinery that makes multiplicative-weights learning converge to NE in potential games simply doesn't apply. Friedman and Shenker showed learning reaches NE in two-player zero-sum games; Daskalakis et al. showed that already breaks at three players. We had no reason to expect a free lunch.</p>
<p>So the head-on attack fails not because of a missing lemma but because the object you'd be solving for has no convenient form.</p>
<h2>The one idea: design the algorithm first, then discover its utility</h2>
<p>Here is the move. Instead of starting from a utility and deriving an algorithm, we <strong>start from an algorithm and reverse-engineer the utility it is secretly maximizing.</strong></p>
<p>The mental image to hold onto: rather than guessing the landscape and rolling a ball down it, we hand-build a ball that rolls the way selfish intuition says it should, then ask <em>what landscape makes this exact motion gradient ascent?</em> If we can recover that landscape, and it turns out to be well-behaved, the equilibrium analysis comes for free.</p>
<p>Concretely, we design a distributed adaptive sensing rule with the right reflexes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Sense <strong>less</strong> when you keep finding the window crowded (back off under congestion).</li>
<li>Sense <strong>more</strong> when you keep catching the server ON (the resource is worth chasing).</li>
<li>Resist change when you sensed too rarely to have learned anything (damping).</li>
</ul>
<p>Crucially this rule needs <em>no</em> knowledge of <span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.10903em;">N</span></span></span></span>, the number of competitors, and no knowledge of anyone else's strategy. A player observes only its own accumulated reward — the congestion it personally experienced, read off as QoS/rate loss. The single quantity it tracks is <span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mover accent="true"><mi>A</mi><mo>^</mo></mover><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>k</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\hat{A}(k)</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1.1968em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord accent"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.9468em;"><span style="top:-3em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:3em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">A</span></span><span style="top:-3.2523em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:3em;"></span><span class="accent-body" style="left:-0.1111em;"><span class="mord">^</span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.03148em;">k</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span>, the empirical average number of players active alongside it (itself included) over the slots of frame <span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>k</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">k</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.03148em;">k</span></span></span></span> in which it was active.</p>

The Twitter scenario shows the <span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>5</mn><mo>→</mo><mn>100</mn><mo>→</mo><mn>5</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">5\to100\to5</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6444em;"></span><span class="mord">5</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">→</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6444em;"></span><span class="mord">100</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">→</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6444em;"></span><span class="mord">5</span></span></span></span> adaptation described above. Both are simulations — parameters drawn from a real testbed, but not a live deployment.</p>

And everything is geometric/Markovian.</p>
<p>If I were to push this further, the prize is the processor-sharing model — letting the service rate <span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>μ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mu</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.625em;vertical-align:-0.1944em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">μ</span></span></span></span> itself degrade with load rather than only the payoff. We avoided it because it couples the dynamics in a way our contraction argument doesn't survive. Relaxing Eq. 2, removing the time-dilation crutch, and moving beyond geometric ON/OFF distributions are the other honest gaps. The settings I most want to see this transplanted into are uplink scheduling with QoS guarantees and device-to-device communication.</p>
<p>The open question I'll leave you with: when payoff <em>and</em> service rate both degrade with congestion, is there still a hand-craftable algorithm whose hidden utility is convex enough to admit a unique equilibrium — or does reverse-engineering the utility stop working precisely when the resource sharing gets realistic?</p>
