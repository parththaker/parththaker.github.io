Why You Can Start Gradient Descent Anywhere — Even on an NP-Hard Recovery Problem

With order-n complex Gaussian quadratic measurements, recovering a vector up to phase becomes both identifiable and solvable by gradient descent from any starting point — two faces of one geometric condition.

phase retrievalnonconvex optimizationquadratic feasibilitysample complexityoptimization landscape

Suppose I hand you a vector x∈Cnx \in \mathbb{C}^nx∈Cn that you cannot see directly. All you get are mmm numbers, each of the form ⟨Aix,x⟩=ci\langle A_i x, x\rangle = c_i⟨Ai​x,x⟩=ci​, where every AiA_iAi​ is a known Hermitian n×nn \times nn×n matrix. Each measurement is a quadratic form — energy-like, sign-blind, and worst of all, blind to global phase: replace xxx by eiφxe^{i\varphi}xeiφx and every cic_ici​ is unchanged. Two questions hang over this immediately. First, is there even enough information to pin down xxx (up to that unavoidable phase)? Second, even if there is, the obvious way to recover it is to minimize a nonconvex loss — and nonconvex least-squares over Cn\mathbb{C}^nCn is the kind of problem that, in general, is NP-hard. So why would any honest person expect to solve it from a cold start?

This is the quadratic feasibility problem, and it is the subject of work I led with my co-authors Gautam Dasarathy and Angelia Nedić at Arizona State. Read the paper · arXiv:2002.01066.

What is actually at stake, and what I am not claiming

Quadratic feasibility — find xxx with ⟨Aix,x⟩=ci\langle A_i x, x\rangle = c_i⟨Ai​x,x⟩=ci​ for all iii — is the feasibility core of a quadratically constrained quadratic program (QCQP). It is not a toy: it models power-system state estimation, x-ray crystallography, the turnpike problem, and unlabeled distance geometry. Its most famous special case is phase retrieval, where each Ai=aiai∗A_i = a_i a_i^*Ai​=ai​ai∗​ is rank one and the measurement collapses to ⟨Aix,x⟩=∣⟨ai,x⟩∣2\langle A_i x, x\rangle = |\langle a_i, x\rangle|^2⟨Ai​x,x⟩=∣⟨ai​,x⟩∣2 — you observe only squared magnitudes. Our setting allows arbitrary, full-rank Hermitian AiA_iAi​.

Let me fence off what I am not doing. I will assume the AiA_iAi​ are complex Gaussian random matrices; I will assume the measurements cic_ici​ are exact (no noise model); and I will give you a landscape result, not a finite-time convergence rate. Those are real limitations and I will come back to them. The payoff in exchange is a clean, two-part statement about when this problem is solvable at all and whether a dumb algorithm solves it.

Accept what phase retrieval already taught us — then find the crack

The "benign nonconvex landscape" program is by now a beautiful body of work. Sun, Qu, and Wright showed that the phase-retrieval ℓ2\ell_2ℓ2​ loss has no spurious local minima and only strict saddles — every local min is global, every critical point that isn't a min has a direction of strict negative curvature. Ge, Jin, and Zheng gave a unified geometric analysis for low-rank problems in the same spirit. So you might think: just cite them and go home.

Here is the crack. Those results live, for the most part, in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, and the real and complex problems are not the same problem with a cosmetic change of field. In the real case, the sign ambiguity x↔−xx \leftrightarrow -xx↔−x gives you exactly two isolated global minima. In the complex case, the phase ambiguity x↔eiφxx \leftrightarrow e^{i\varphi}xx↔eiφx gives you a whole continuum of equivalent global minima. A landscape proof that counts isolated minima, or that leans on real differentiability, simply does not transport. Separately, Huang, Gupta, and Dokmanić showed complex quadratic systems with full-rank random matrices are solvable by gradient descent — but only from a carefully chosen spectral initialization. The question I cared about: can you drop the clever initialization entirely?

Why the obvious recovery objective fights back

The obvious move is to minimize
f(x)=1m∑i=1m∣⟨Aix,x⟩−ci∣2,f(x) = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m \big|\langle A_i x, x\rangle - c_i\big|^2,f(x)=m1​∑i=1m​​⟨Ai​x,x⟩−ci​​2,
the squared residual, with f≥0f \ge 0f≥0 and f=0f = 0f=0 exactly at feasible points. Two things go wrong at once. The loss is nonconvex, so a priori there could be spurious local minima where gradient descent gets stuck. And fff is not complex-differentiable — it depends on both xxx and xˉ\bar{x}xˉ — so ordinary calculus on Cn\mathbb{C}^nCn does not even hand us a gradient to descend on. Lurking underneath both is the prior question of whether f=0f = 0f=0 has an essentially unique answer at all.

The one idea: measure distance the way the problem does

Here is the move the whole paper rests on. Stop measuring how far apart xxx and yyy are with ∥x−y∥\|x - y\|∥x−y∥ — that metric is offended by phase, reporting a large distance between xxx and eiφxe^{i\varphi}xeiφx even though they are the same point for us. Instead use the phase-invariant distance
d(x,y)=∥xx∗−yy∗∥F.d(x,y) = \|xx^* - yy^*\|_F.d(x,y)=∥xx∗−yy∗∥F​.
Here xx∗xx^*xx∗ is the rank-one n×nn\times nn×n Hermitian matrix (the "lifted" version of xxx), and ∥⋅∥F\|\cdot\|_F∥⋅∥F​ is the Frobenius norm. The single fact that makes this the right yardstick: d(x,y)=0d(x,y) = 0d(x,y)=0 if and only if x=eiφyx = e^{i\varphi}yx=eiφy for some phase φ\varphiφ. The metric is zero exactly on the orbits we cannot and should not distinguish.

The mental image to keep: lift every vector to its outer product, and do all your geometry up there, where phase has already been quotiented out. Once you are in the world of xx∗xx^*xx∗, "recover xxx up to phase" becomes "recover the matrix xx∗xx^*xx∗," and the awkward symmetry evaporates.

xx∗−yy∗=[ ⁣[u,v] ⁣].xx^* - yy^* = [\![u,v]\!].xx∗−yy∗=[[u,v]].
This converts "tell xxx and yyy apart" into "the AiA_iAi​ must not crush the rank-2 Hermitian matrix [ ⁣[u,v] ⁣][\![u,v]\!][[u,v]]." That is a statement about how a linear map — the AiA_iAi​ acting on matrices — treats low-rank matrices, exactly the territory where RIP-style isometry arguments live.

From unbiasedness to identifiability: an isometry

Call the measurement map MA(x)=(⟨A1x,x⟩,…,⟨Amx,x⟩)M_A(x) = (\langle A_1 x, x\rangle, \dots, \langle A_m x, x\rangle)MA​(x)=(⟨A1​x,x⟩,…,⟨Am​x,x⟩). Say MAM_AMA​ is (α,β)(\alpha,\beta)(α,β)-stable if
α d(x1,x2)  ≤  ∥MA(x1)−MA(x2)∥2  ≤  β d(x1,x2).\alpha\, d(x_1,x_2) \;\le\; \|M_A(x_1) - M_A(x_2)\|_2 \;\le\; \beta\, d(x_1,x_2).αd(x1​,x2​)≤∥MA​(x1​)−MA​(x2​)∥2​≤βd(x1​,x2​).
The structural fact, via Wang and Xu's phase-retrievability characterization: MAM_AMA​ is injective up to phase if and only if it is (α,β)(\alpha,\beta)(α,β)-stable. Identifiability is a RIP-like sandwich in the metric ddd — not analogous to one, literally the same statement. The same condition makes the landscape benign

Now the second face. Because fff is not holomorphic, we differentiate with Wirtinger calculus — treat xxx and xˉ\bar xxˉ as independent variables. The goal is to rule out spurious minima, and the technique is to exhibit strict negative curvature everywhere a non-solution could hide: at every point xxx with small gradient there is a direction Δ\DeltaΔ with
⟨∇2f(x) Δ,Δ⟩  ≤  −c0 ∥xx∗−zz∗∥F2  <  0whenever d(x,z)>0,\langle \nabla^2 f(x)\, \Delta, \Delta\rangle \;\le\; -c_0\, \|xx^* - zz^*\|_F^2 \;<\; 0 \quad\text{whenever } d(x,z) > 0,⟨∇2f(x)Δ,Δ⟩≤−c0​∥xx∗−zz∗∥F2​<0whenever d(x,z)>0,
where zzz is the ground truth generating the cic_ici​. Notice the right-hand side is −c0 d(x,z)2-c_0\, d(x,z)^2−c0​d(x,z)2 — the same phase-invariant distance, again doing the bookkeeping. Strict negative curvature at every non-optimal stationary point means no flat traps.

Theorem 2 (benign landscape). For complex Gaussian AiA_iAi​ with m>Cnm > Cnm>Cn, with probability ≥1−ξ\ge 1-\xi≥1−ξ: fff is strict-saddle, and every local minimum www satisfies d(w,z)=0d(w,z)=0d(w,z)=0. No spurious local minima; every local min is global up to phase. Corollary. A gradient method from an arbitrary initialization therefore converges to a global minimum, via the saddle-avoidance results of Lee, Simchowitz, Jordan, and Recht.

The one sentence to keep for a week: identifiability and tractable optimization are governed by one and the same geometric condition — the (α,β)(\alpha,\beta)(α,β)-stability that complex Gaussian measurements buy you at m≳nm \gtrsim nm≳n.

What the result really says, and where it goes vacuous

The defended claim, with its boundary: order-nnn Gaussian quadratic measurements suffice for both identifiability and a globally optimizable landscape, with no clever initialization. That last clause is the sharp contrast with Huang–Gupta–Dokmanić, who needed a spectral start.

Now the honest limits. The guarantees are for complex Gaussian AiA_iAi​ — not structured, deterministic, or coded measurements. The constants (CCC, α\alphaα, β\betaβ, c0c_0c0​) are existence-type and unoptimized; m>Cnm > Cnm>Cn is order-nnn, not a sharp threshold, and pushing δ,ε\delta,\varepsilonδ,ε smaller (stronger stability) inflates CCC — a real tradeoff. The corollary gives asymptotic saddle-avoidance, not a finite-time rate. There is no noise model: cic_ici​ are exact. And to be candid, this manuscript is theory-focused; I would not have you take a benchmark number from me here.

The load-bearing assumption is the Gaussianity, and it is exactly where the result threatens to go vacuous: every "with high probability" rides on the second-moment identity E∣⟨Ai,xx∗−yy∗⟩∣2=d(x,y)2\mathbb{E}|\langle A_i, xx^*-yy^*\rangle|^2 = d(x,y)^2E∣⟨Ai​,xx∗−yy∗⟩∣2=d(x,y)2 and on the concentration bound above. Break those — heavy tails, strong structure, adversarial AiA_iAi​ — and neither the isometry nor the curvature lower bound is guaranteed.

What I would do next

The cleanest open question: how far can the Gaussian assumption be relaxed — to sub-Gaussian, coded-diffraction, or sparse AiA_iAi​ — while keeping the landscape benign? After that: the tight sample-complexity constant (is m≍nm \asymp nm≍n optimal?); finite-time iteration complexity to replace the qualitative convergence; a noise/robustness model with stable recovery bounds; and exploiting structure (sparse or low-rank xxx). If you take one thing away, let it be the reframing: lift to xx∗xx^*xx∗, measure with ddd, and identifiability and optimizability turn out to be the same fact seen twice.
